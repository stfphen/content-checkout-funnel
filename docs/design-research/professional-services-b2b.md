# Landing-Page Conversion Patterns: Professional Services B2B

Research notes for consulting, legal, accounting, and funding-advisory funnels. Patterns only, no copied code or copy. Compiled 2026-07-03.

## 1. Section archetypes that convert, canonical order

This vertical is trust-first. Proof and risk-reduction appear before any offer or price. Observed canonical rhythm (Pilot, Grants Plus, Grant Management Associates, Morgan & Morgan, Bain):

1. **Outcome hero with one CTA** (`hero`). Who you help, the outcome, one primary action. A trust signal sits inside or directly under the hero (client count, aggregate dollar figure, media badges).
2. **Immediate proof strip** (`references`, partial). Client logos or a stat banner within the first viewport scroll. Comes before any explanation of services.
3. **Problem framing** (`problem`). Names the prospect's pain in their words (buried in bookkeeping, missed funding, insurance lowball). Best copy here competes with "should I hire anyone at all," not with rival firms.
4. **Method / approach** (`system` then `process`). A named methodology or 3-step engagement workflow. Process transparency is itself a trust device in this vertical; every strong site had one.
5. **Outcomes and case studies** (`output` then `portfolio`). Specific engagements with dollar figures and named clients. Verdict cards, funding awards, before/after metrics.
6. **Named testimonials and team credentials** (`references`). Quotes with full name, title, company. Advisor bios with headshots and credentials (CPA, bar, WOSB certification).
7. **Engagement options, then low-friction close** (`packages`, `checkout`, `faq`, `finalCta`). Most firms hide exact pricing and sell a consultation; a productized funnel can show packages here because trust was already built. FAQ handles risk objections (fees, timelines, guarantees). Final CTA repeats the hero action, often with an inline form or scheduler.

Key deviation from SaaS: logos and proof move UP (viewport 1-2), pricing moves DOWN or off-page, and a process section is mandatory rather than optional.

## 2. Three hero patterns

**A. Split hero with credible human photo.** Text column left (55-60 percent): eyebrow line, 6-10 word headline, one-sentence subhead, single CTA, small proof line beneath. Right column: candid photo of the actual principal or team mid-work, not stock. Headline around 3x body size, not oversized. Most common in boutique consulting and grant firms (Grants Plus pattern).

**B. Centered statement hero with proof banner.** One centered question-or-claim headline, subhead, single button, then a full-width stat or logo band directly below acting as the hero's floor. Type is the asset; imagery deferred to section 2. Works when the aggregate number is the strongest asset ($2.5B secured, 3,000+ clients). Pilot and Grant Management Associates variants.

**C. Full-bleed environmental image with left-anchored text block.** Cinematic photo or subtle video of a real workspace or city context, dark scrim, compact text block anchored left with headline, subhead, CTA, and a badge row. Larger type scale (display size), short headline (4-7 words). Big-firm register (Bain, large legal). Needs excellent photography or it collapses into stock-slop.

## 3. Trust and social-proof patterns for this vertical

- **Aggregate dollar outcomes**: one big cumulative number ($450M raised, $35B recovered) placed early, then individual engagement outcomes ($4M award, $37.8M grant) as cards later. Two altitudes of the same proof.
- **Win-rate or multiplier framing**: 90.2 percent win rate, "48x the initial offer." Comparative framing beats raw counts.
- **Credential badges**: bar admissions, CPA, WOSB/WBE certification, institute memberships, "as seen in" media logos. Real law firms give third-party editorial validation the most visual weight, placed near CTAs.
- **Named testimonials with title and company**, ideally sector-diverse (Grants Plus shows six across healthcare, education, arts, legal). Anonymous quotes read as fabricated here.
- **Team as proof**: advisor bios with headshots, credentials, and tenure. Buyers hire people, not platforms.
- **Process transparency**: a numbered engagement workflow with what happens at each step and when. Reduces perceived risk of an opaque retainer.
- **Placement rule**: put a trust element adjacent to every CTA, so the moment of decision is also a moment of reassurance.

## 4. Copy register

- **Tone**: measured, specific, zero hype. Declarative and grounded in outcomes. Partnership language (we listen, we invest ourselves) over conquest language. Confidence without superlatives.
- **Headlines**: 4-10 words. Problem-questions work (Pilot's "who's leading the company?" pattern). Avoid abstract vision statements.
- **CTA verbs**: "Book a consultation," "Talk to our team," "See our results," "Get a free case evaluation," "Request a proposal." Avoid "Get started," "Sign up," "Try free," which read SaaS and cheapen an advisory offer. One primary action per page.
- **Specificity beats adjectives**: name the dollar figure, the timeline, the deliverable. "Nearly $450 million since 2007" outperforms "proven results."
- **Jargon to avoid**: synergy, leverage (as verb), solutions, best-in-class, world-class, cutting-edge, holistic, seamless, unlock, empower. Sector terms (go/no-go, retainer, contingency) are fine; buyers know them.

## 5. Imagery brief

- **Subjects**: the actual principals working (whiteboards, documents, client conversations), real office environments, close document/detail shots, city or civic context for legal and funding. People should be identifiable team members, never models.
- **Aspect ratios**: hero 16:9 or 3:2; split-hero portrait column 4:5; case-study cards 3:2; headshots 1:1; logo strips on transparent backgrounds at uniform optical height.
- **Photographic vs illustrative**: photographic dominates this vertical. Illustration only as small diagrammatic support (process steps, system maps), never as hero decoration.
- Direction flexes:
  - **premium-agency** (cinematic full-bleed, Geist): desaturated environmental photography, heavy scrims, one image per section maximum, motion restraint.
  - **editorial-minimal** (warm monochrome, serif, hairlines): duotone or warm-grayscale photos, small and precisely placed, generous margins, captions in small caps.
  - **bold-brutalist** (Swiss print, uppercase, 2px rules): high-contrast black-and-white photography cropped hard into the grid, stats set as giant type instead of images.
  - **warm-boutique** (creams, rounded, soft shadows): natural-light candid photos with warm grade, rounded-corner masks, human faces prioritized over environments.
  - **dark-cinematic** (OLED dark, glass, serif): low-key photography with single light source, images behind glass panels, gold or warm-metal accents on credentials.

## 6. Anti-patterns and AI-slop tells

- Stock handshake, gavel-and-scales, or boardroom-of-models photos. Instantly reads as template filler.
- Fake-precise stats: "trusted by 500+ businesses" with no logos, "98 percent satisfaction" with no source. If a number cannot be attributed, cut it.
- Purple-to-blue gradient heroes, glassmorphism cards on gradients, floating 3D blobs. Codes as SaaS template, wrong register for advisory.
- Em-dashes scattered through copy, triadic "Not X. Not Y. Just Z." constructions, and "In today's fast-paced world" openers.
- Three-tier pricing tables with a "Most popular" badge for bespoke advisory work. Package by engagement scope instead.
- Generic icon rows (rocket, lightbulb, shield) explaining services. Use numbered process steps or real artifacts.
- Chat widgets and multiple competing CTAs above the fold. One action, one direction.
- Emoji in headings or body copy anywhere on a professional-services page.
